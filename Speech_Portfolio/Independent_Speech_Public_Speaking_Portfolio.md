# SECTION 2 — TOPIC & OBJECTIVE

### Final Speech Topic
**"Why Financial Literacy Is the Most Important Skill for My Generation."**

### Why This Topic Was Selected
As a BFM student, I am surrounded by markets, investing, and financial decisions every day. But outside the classroom, I see a different reality. Most students my age know how to use ten different apps but cannot explain what an SIP, an index fund, or a credit score actually is. I chose this topic because it sits exactly between **what I am studying** and **what my generation actually needs to hear.** It is also a topic I can speak about honestly — not as an expert, but as a student who is learning the same things I am asking my audience to learn.

### Objective of the Speech
- To explain what financial literacy means in simple, relatable language.
- To highlight why young Indians, especially students, must learn it early.
- To inspire the audience to take **one small financial action** after listening.

### Intended Audience
- College students (16–22 years old)
- BFM, BBA, B.Com, and general degree students
- Faculty and internship interviewers reviewing this portfolio

### What the Audience Should Learn
1. Financial literacy is not "advanced finance" — it is a **life skill**.
2. Starting early is more powerful than starting big.
3. Three habits — **Track, Save, Invest** — are enough to begin with.
4. The best time to learn money is **before** you start earning it.

# SECTION 3 — FULL SPEECH SCRIPT (5–7 Minutes)

> **Length:** ~870 spoken words → roughly 6 minutes at a calm, confident pace.
> **Notation:** *(pause)* = short breath, **bold** = emphasis, *italics* = softer/personal tone.

---

### INTRODUCTION — *The Hook* (≈ 60 seconds)

Good morning, everyone. *(pause)*

Before I begin, I want to ask you a quick question. Just answer it in your head — you don't have to say it out loud.

**How much money did you spend last month?** *(pause)*

Now a harder one — **where did it actually go?** *(pause)*

If your honest answer is *"I have no idea,"* don't worry. You are not alone. In fact, you are completely normal. *(pause)*

A survey by the National Centre for Financial Education found that only about **27% of Indians are financially literate.** That means roughly three out of every four people around you — your friends, your cousins, even some adults you look up to — are making money decisions without really understanding money.

And that is exactly why I am standing here today. *(pause)*

My name is [Your Name], I am a Bachelor of Financial Markets student, and I want to talk to you about something I genuinely believe is **the most important skill of our generation** — *financial literacy.*

---

### TRANSITION

So let me ask the obvious question first — *what does financial literacy even mean?*

---

### MAIN POINT 1 — *What Financial Literacy Actually Is* (≈ 75 seconds)

When most people hear the word **"finance,"** they imagine the stock market, traders shouting on TV, or some uncle at a family function giving unsolicited advice about gold. *(pause for light laughter)*

But financial literacy is **much simpler than that.**

In one line — financial literacy is the ability to **understand, manage, and grow your money confidently.**

That's it.

It is knowing the difference between a *need* and a *want.*
It is understanding what an **SIP**, a **credit score**, or an **emergency fund** actually does.
It is being able to read your own bank statement without panicking.

It is **not** about becoming Warren Buffett. *(pause)*
It is about not becoming the person who reaches the end of the month and asks, *"Where did my salary go?"*

And here is the part that surprised me when I started studying BFM — **none of this is taught in school.** We learn trigonometry, we learn the periodic table, but we don't learn what an EMI is until we are signing one.

---

### TRANSITION

Which brings me to my second point — *why this matters so much for people our age.*

---

### MAIN POINT 2 — *Why It Matters Right Now* (≈ 90 seconds)

We are the **first fully digital money generation** in India. *(pause)*

Think about it. Our parents handled cash. We handle UPI. We send money in two seconds, we shop at midnight, we invest from our phones in three taps. Our money moves **faster than our understanding of it.**

And that is dangerous.

Because the same phone that lets you start an SIP also lets you lose ₹10,000 on a "guaranteed tip" from a random Telegram group. *(pause)* The same app that helps you save can push you into a loan you don't need.

Add one more thing to this — **inflation.** The samosa that cost ₹10 when we were kids costs ₹25 today. *(pause)* If your money is just sitting in a savings account, it is **slowly losing value every single year.**

So the question is not *"Should I learn about money?"*
The question is *"Can I afford not to?"*

And here is the most beautiful part — **time is on our side.** If a 19-year-old invests just ₹2,000 a month at a reasonable return, by the time they are 45, that small habit can grow into something genuinely life-changing. *(pause)*

Not because of magic. Because of **compounding** — and compounding rewards the people who **start early,** not the people who start big.

---

### TRANSITION

So if you're sitting there thinking, *"Okay, I get it, but where do I even start?"* — let me make it really simple.

---

### MAIN POINT 3 — *Three Habits To Start With* (≈ 75 seconds)

You don't need a finance degree. You don't need a Demat account on day one. You only need **three habits.** I call them **Track, Save, Invest.** *(emphasis)*

**One — Track.**
For 30 days, just write down every rupee you spend. No judgment, no budget yet. Just awareness. You will be shocked how much disappears into food delivery and "small" online orders.

**Two — Save.**
Follow a simple rule — the **50-30-20 rule.** 50% on needs, 30% on wants, 20% on savings. Even if you are a student with pocket money, save **something.** The amount is not the point. The habit is.

**Three — Invest.**
Once you have a small emergency fund, start a **SIP of even ₹500** in an index fund. Read about it before you do it. Make mistakes with small money now, so you don't make them with big money later.

That's it. *(pause)* **Track. Save. Invest.** Three words. One life skill.

---

### CONCLUSION — *The Closing* (≈ 60 seconds)

Let me bring this back to where we started. *(pause)*

I asked you what you spent last month and where it went. By next month — I hope at least a few of you can actually answer that question.

Because financial literacy is **not** about getting rich quickly. It is about **getting in control quietly.** *(pause for emphasis)*

It is the difference between money working for you, and you working only for money.

We are young. We don't have huge salaries yet. But we have something more powerful than any salary — **time, curiosity, and the internet.** And that is more than enough to start.

So when you walk out of this room today, I am not asking you to open a brokerage account or read a finance book tonight. I am only asking you to do **one thing** — *track every rupee you spend for the next seven days.*

That one small habit is where my financial journey started. *(pause)* And I genuinely believe it is where yours can begin too.

Thank you. *(pause, smile, eye contact)*

---

# SECTION 6 — VIDEO RECORDING GUIDE

> *Designed for a phone-based recording done in your room or college classroom.*

### 1. Camera Setup
- Use a phone with at least 1080p video.
- Mount it on a tripod, books, or a stable surface at **eye level** — never below your chin.
- Frame yourself from **mid-chest to just above the head.**
- Keep the slide screen visible **next to you** if possible (split screen can be added later in editing).

### 2. Lighting
- Face a window during the daytime — natural light is the best free upgrade.
- If recording at night: use a ring light or a plain white wall + a desk lamp pointed at the ceiling.
- Avoid backlight (window behind you = silhouette).

### 3. Audio
- Use **earphones with a mic** or a basic clip-on lavalier (₹300–₹600 ones work fine).
- Record in a quiet room — close windows, switch off the fan if it's noisy.
- Do a 10-second test recording before the real take.

### 4. Body Language
- Stand, don't sit. Standing makes your voice more confident.
- Keep shoulders relaxed, feet shoulder-width apart.
- Don't lock your knees or sway side to side.

### 5. Hand Gestures
- Use your hands at chest level, not below the waist.
- Open palms = trust. Pointing fingers = aggressive (avoid).
- Match gestures to keywords: e.g., "three habits" — show three fingers.

### 6. Eye Contact
- Look directly into the **camera lens**, not at your own face on the screen.
- Imagine one specific friend on the other side of the lens. Speak to them.

### 7. Speaking Speed
- Aim for ~140–150 words per minute.
- **Pauses are powerful.** Use them after questions and big statements.
- If you stumble, **don't apologize** — pause, breathe, and continue.

### 8. Confidence Tips
- Do **two warm-up reads** before the final take.
- Smile slightly *before* you hit record — it carries into your voice.
- Power-pose for 60 seconds before recording (hands on hips, head up). Sounds silly. Works.

### 9. What to Wear
- Solid colors only — navy, white, light blue, beige, soft maroon.
- Avoid logos, busy patterns, neon shades, and pure black on dark backgrounds.
- A blazer over a plain t-shirt or shirt looks internship-ready without being stiff.
- Hair neat, face clean. Minimal accessories.

### 10. Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Reading directly from a script in front of the camera.
- Speaking in a flat, monotonous tone.
- Filling silence with "um," "like," "basically," "you know."
- Cropping your forehead out of the frame.
- Recording in a vertical phone orientation — **always shoot horizontal (landscape).**
- Forgetting to switch the phone to **Do Not Disturb** before recording.

### 11. Final Take Checklist
- [ ] Tripod stable, phone horizontal
- [ ] Light on face, not behind
- [ ] Mic plugged in and tested
- [ ] DND mode on
- [ ] Slide deck open as backup
- [ ] Water nearby
- [ ] Two practice runs done

# SECTION 7 — SELF-EVALUATION

### Strengths Demonstrated
- **Topic clarity:** I picked a topic I genuinely care about, which made the speech sound authentic rather than rehearsed.
- **Structure:** The speech followed a clear three-point structure — *What, Why Now, How* — which made it easy for the audience to follow.

### Areas Needing Improvement
- **Hand gestures** still feel slightly stiff in the first minute. I tend to keep my hands too close to my body before I "warm up."
- **Eye contact** during the middle section drifts toward the slide screen instead of staying with the audience.
- **Filler words** — I caught myself using "basically" and "actually" more than I'd like. I am consciously working on replacing them with pauses.
- **Confidence in the opening 15 seconds** — this is where nervousness shows up the most, and it's the most important part of any speech.

### Confidence Level
On a 1–10 scale, my **current confidence rating is 7/10.** I am comfortable with the content and the structure, and I no longer freeze mid-sentence. The remaining 3 points come down to physical presence — voice projection, gestures, and stage stillness — which only improve with repetition.

The slide deck genuinely supported the speech instead of competing with it. I deliberately kept slides minimal so that the audience would listen to me, not read past me. The strongest slides were Slide 3 (the 27% statistic) and Slide 7 (Track / Save / Invest), because they gave the audience something visual to anchor onto.

### Audience Engagement Reflection
The opening question — *"How much did you spend last month?"* — worked well as a hook because I could see the audience pause and actually think. The samosa-inflation example got smiles, which lowered the formality of the room. If I were to do this speech again, I would add **one more direct question** in the middle to keep engagement consistent throughout, instead of only at the start and end.

# SECTION 9 — REFLECTION

### Why I Chose This Topic
I chose financial literacy because it is the rare topic that sits **inside my course** and **inside my generation's daily life** at the same time. Most of my classmates can name three IPL teams faster than three mutual funds. I didn't want to give a speech that sounded impressive in a classroom and useless outside of it. I wanted to talk about something that could actually change one habit in one listener — and financial literacy felt like that topic.

### Challenges During Preparation
The hardest part wasn't writing the speech. It was **deciding what to leave out.** As a BFM student, I had the temptation to talk about beta, alpha, equity research, technical analysis, valuation — all the things that make me sound smart. But every time I added jargon, the speech became colder. I had to keep cutting until what was left actually sounded like me, not like a textbook.

The second challenge was **timing.** My first draft was over 9 minutes. Cutting it to under 7 without losing meaning forced me to write tighter sentences — a skill I now realise applies to emails, interviews, and even research notes.

### The Speaking Experience Itself
Standing up to deliver this was honestly nerve-wracking the first few times. My voice was faster than I wanted it to be, and I could feel my hands looking for something to hold. By the third rehearsal, the words had moved from my notes into my memory, and that's when I started actually *speaking* instead of *reciting.*

The recorded final take felt different — calmer, slower, more eye contact, fewer fillers. Watching myself back was uncomfortable, but it was also the single most useful learning moment of this entire project.

### Nervousness vs. Confidence
I used to think nervousness meant I was unprepared. Through this project I learned that nervousness mostly means **the topic matters to you.** That reframe changed everything. Now, before I begin, I take three slow breaths and tell myself: *"You're not nervous. You just care."* It works almost every time.

### Lessons Learned
- A speech is a **conversation,** not a performance.
- The audience does not care about your slides. They care about whether you sound believable.
- The opening 15 seconds and the final 15 seconds are 80% of what people remember.
- Pauses are not silence — they are emphasis.
- Preparation kills nervousness faster than confidence ever will.

### Future Improvement Goals
- Speak in **at least one finance-related college event per semester.**
- Sit for a mock equity-pitch presentation and get faculty/peer feedback.
- Reduce filler words from the current 4–5 per minute to under 2 per minute.
- Build a small library of 5 strong, ready-to-deliver finance talks for internship interviews and college events.
- Eventually, present a research-based pitch on a real listed company in front of a finance club or panel.
